Hack Wall – Types of Walls That Can and Cannot Be Hacked

Hacking a wall refers to the demolition or partial removal of a wall using tools such as a pneumatic drill, angle grinder or electric chipping hammer. It is a common part of renovation and reinstatement works in Singapore — but not all walls can be hacked. Understanding which walls are structural and which are non-structural is critical before any demolition work begins.

Types of Walls That Can Typically Be Hacked

  • Lightweight partition walls — plasterboard, gypsum board or metal stud partitions installed during a previous renovation. These carry no structural load and can be removed.
  • Brick partition walls — non-load-bearing brick walls used to divide internal spaces. These can be hacked but require more effort due to the material weight and debris volume.
  • Timber and glass partitions — typically installed during commercial fit-outs, these are non-structural and straightforward to dismantle.
  • Tile and screed layers — floor and wall tiles along with the screed bed underneath can be hacked when retiling or repairing the substrate.

Walls That Must Not Be Hacked

  • Structural concrete walls — these form part of the building's load-bearing system. Hacking or penetrating these walls without engineering approval can compromise the structural integrity of the building.
  • Shear walls — common in high-rise HDB blocks and condominiums, shear walls resist lateral forces such as wind loads. They must never be modified without professional assessment.
  • Party walls between units — walls shared with an adjacent unit typically have fire-rated requirements and structural roles. Any modification requires approval.

How to Tell if a Wall Is Structural

A simple knock test can give a rough indication — hollow walls tend to be non-structural partitions, while solid walls are more likely structural. However, this is not definitive. For HDB flats, the floor plan provided by HDB at purchase clearly indicates which walls are structural. For condominiums and landed properties, as-built drawings from the developer or a structural engineer's assessment should be consulted.
