Wall Hacking Singapore – HDB Regulations, Permits and Structural Rules

Wall hacking in Singapore is governed by specific regulations depending on the property type. HDB flats, condominiums and landed properties each have different rules regarding what can be demolished, what requires approval and what is strictly prohibited. Understanding these rules before starting renovation work protects you from fines, enforcement action and structural risk.

HDB Wall Hacking Rules

HDB has clear guidelines on which walls in a flat can be hacked and which are protected. All HDB flats have a set of structural walls — indicated on the floor plan — that form the load-bearing framework of the block. These walls cannot be hacked, modified or penetrated without written approval from HDB and, in most cases, a structural engineer's assessment.

Non-structural internal partition walls — typically thinner brick or lightweight partitions — can generally be removed as part of renovation. However, all renovation works in HDB flats must be carried out by a contractor registered under HDB's Renovation Contractor Registration (RCR) scheme.

HDB Renovation Permit

For works involving wall hacking in HDB flats, a renovation permit must be obtained from HDB before works commence. The permit application is typically submitted by the registered renovation contractor on behalf of the homeowner. Works that require a permit include:

  • Hacking of internal walls
  • Removal of floor or wall tiles
  • Replacement of main door or bedroom doors
  • Electrical rewiring works

Carrying out works without a permit can result in a fine and an order to reinstate the original condition at the owner's expense.

Condominium Wall Hacking

For private condominiums, wall hacking is subject to the building's house rules and the management corporation's (MCST) approval. Most MCSTes require homeowners to submit renovation plans, obtain a renovation permit from the MCST and pay a refundable renovation deposit before works begin. Structural modifications typically require additional approval from a qualified person (QP) such as a structural engineer.

Landed Property

For landed properties, internal non-structural partitions can generally be removed without formal approval. However, works affecting the external envelope of the building — including load-bearing walls — may require a building permit from the Building and Construction Authority (BCA) and submission by a qualified person.
